FAQs

Good questions, quick answers.

Everything visitors ask most, in one place.

What are the hours, and does it cost anything?
The park is open dawn to dusk, every day of the year, and admission is always free.
Where should I park?
Four lots serve the park: the Dryad Drive Lot (5300 Dryad Dr, the main entrance, closest to Westbury Lake), two Clematis Lane lots (11200 and 11300 blocks, closest to Willow and Heron Lakes), and the Gasmer Drive Lot (4700 block, closest to Prairie Lake and the boardwalk). See Getting Here & Parking for which lot fits your visit.
Are there restrooms?
No — there are no restrooms on site, so plan ahead. The nearby Dr. Walker Library offers indoor space and restrooms.
Can I bring my dog?
Yes! Dogs are welcome on leash. Please keep them leashed at all times and use the waste stations.
Can I fish?
Yes. Four lakes — Westbury, Willow, Prairie, and Triangle — were stocked by Texas Parks & Wildlife with bass, catfish, and sunfish. Carp are invasive; please remove any you catch. Fishing poles are available to borrow — email ahead.
Can I launch a kayak?
Yes, when conditions allow. Most people launch at Westbury Lake near the fishing pier — the park's largest lake, with calm water for short, easy paddling.
Are there alligators?
No. Old warning signs were removed because they gave the wrong impression — there are no alligators in the lakes.
Is the park accessible?
Yes — eight miles of ADA-accessible trails connect the lakes, and the fishing pier at Westbury Lake is ADA-compliant.
When is the best time to see birds?
Early morning and late afternoon, year-round. Spring and fall migration bring the biggest variety. A monthly bird survey runs the third Saturday of every month (except December) — all levels welcome. See Birds & Wildlife.
How do I volunteer?
Workdays run monthly for individuals, groups, scouts, and corporate teams. Every volunteer signs a simple waiver before their first workday. Start at Get Involved or email info@willowwaterhole.org.
Is the park really a flood-control project?
Yes — the six lakes are stormwater detention basins that can hold 608 million gallons, engineered by the Harris County Flood Control District as part of Project Brays. The Conservancy stewards the greenspace on top. Read How the lakes reduce flooding.
