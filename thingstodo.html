Don't Miss

The landmarks everyone asks about.

Turtle Bridge

The park's most loved spot. Watch turtles gather below the rail — corn or peas only, never bread.

Bob Schwartz Gazebo

The community's gathering place since 2003, and the stage for concerts and celebrations by Westbury Lake.

Great Blue Heron Sculpture

Heron Lake's newly installed landmark, standing watch over the park's best birding water.

Rafferty Memorial Oak

An ancient live oak at Triangle Lake honoring Jack Rafferty — a shady landmark generations old.

Native Prairie Garden

A pocket showcase of coastal prairie plants at the park's "front porch," buzzing with pollinators.

Birding Center

Near the Arboretum Trail at Triangle Lake, with chimney swift habitat and the start of many bird walks.
Now Open

The Prairie Boardwalk.

Top of every list: a half-mile elevated boardwalk into a 20-acre native coastal prairie, one of the last of its kind in Texas, with 265 native plant species and interpretive panels the whole way.

The Six Lakes.

See the full park map →

Start at any lake and follow the trails wherever they lead. Each has its own named trail, character, and views.

01 · Trail A

Westbury Lake

Kevin Shanley Trail · ~2.2 mi loopADA pavedFishing pier

The park's central gathering space and the largest, most visited lake in the Greenway. Stroll the loop, cast a line off the ADA-compliant fishing pier, rest at the Bob Schwartz Gazebo, climb the two-tier Overlook Trail, find the James D. Phillips tree carvings (say hello to "Buster the Dog"), and finish at the beloved Turtle Bridge.

02 · Trail B

Triangle Lake

Brown Foundation / Arboretum Trail · 0.9 miNative garden

The park's welcoming "front porch," home to the landmark Willow Waterhole sign, a native prairie garden, and an ancient live oak. The Dabney Kennedy Scout Pavilion honors more than 80 Eagle and Gold Scout projects, the Birding Center sits nearby with its chimney swift habitat, and stone seating forms an outdoor classroom.

03 · Trail C

Scout Lake

O'Quinn Foundation / Bayou Trail · 0.4 miShaded & quiet

The smallest lake, named for the scout projects that shaped it. Quiet, shaded shorelines meet the bayou here, and the visible concrete weir shows the Greenway's stormwater engineering up close — a favorite short walk for birdwatchers.

04 · Trail D

Heron Lake

Audubon Trail · 0.5 miTop birding spot

Slow down, settle in, and watch the waterbirds from a shaded bench beneath a massive, ancient oak. Named for the herons that nest and forage here, and home to the park's new Great Blue Heron sculpture.

05 · Trail E

Willow Lake

Kinder Foundation Trail · 1.4 miWetlands

Expansive waters and vibrant wetlands at the heart of the Greenway. This deep-water haven is a premier destination for migratory waterbirds, from spoonbills to herons, and one of the best places in the park to watch birds move across the water in the early morning.

06 · Trail F

Prairie Lake

Cullen Foundation Trail · 1.5 miPrairie gateway

Across South Post Oak Road, Prairie Lake is your gateway to the coastal prairie and the new boardwalk, with front-row seats to Houston's spring wildflower displays. Fifteen young Loblolly Pines, planted in 2025, are growing along the trail.

Activities

Many ways to spend a day.

Walk, hike & run

Flat, accessible trails connect the lakes and natural areas. No required loop — explore at your own pace.

Bike

Smooth, shared trails link all six lakes. Ride at a friendly pace and give walkers a heads-up when passing.

Fish

Four wet-bottom lakes — Westbury, Willow, Prairie, and Triangle — were stocked by Texas Parks & Wildlife with bass, catfish, and sunfish. Carp are invasive; please remove any you catch.

Paddle

When conditions allow, calm water on Westbury Lake makes for short, easy paddling. Most people launch near the fishing pier.

Picnic & relax

Shaded areas and open green space make ideal picnic spots. Settle near the water or along a quiet side path.

Hammock & do nothing

String a hammock, find a bench, watch the water. Doing nothing here is a valid — even recommended — way to visit.

Escape Without Leaving Houston

Forest breathing, in five minutes.

Find a quiet spot near the water. Stand or sit comfortably. Breathe in slowly through your nose for four counts, hold for four, and release for six. Let your eyes rest on the farthest thing you can see — the treeline, the water, the sky. Repeat for five minutes.

That's it. The park does the rest.

Birds & Wildlife

One of Houston's great urban birding spots.

Willow Waterhole sits on the Great Texas Coastal Birding Trail, a stopover on the Central Flyway where migratory birds rest and feed each spring and fall. Its mix of open water, wetlands, prairie, and woodland draws an extraordinary range of species into one walkable greenway — more than 250 recorded to date. A December 2023 survey counted 236 species, the highest of any official Houston park.

Roseate Spoonbill

The park's showstopper. These vivid pink waders feed in the shallows and are one of Willow Waterhole's signature sights.

Herons & Egrets

Great blue herons, egrets, and other waders nest and forage along the shorelines — Heron Lake is named for them, and Willow Lake's wetlands shelter a working rookery.

Migratory Visitors

Bald eagles, ospreys, warblers, and many duck species pass through on migration each spring and fall.

Join the monthly bird survey

Third Saturday of every month (except December) at The Gathering Place lot, organized with the Nature Discovery Center and Houston Audubon. Around 50 species per outing — beginners genuinely welcome.

Best times to bird

Early morning and late afternoon, year-round. Spring and fall migration bring the biggest variety; winter brings the ducks.

Where to start

The Clematis lots for quiet wildlife viewing at Willow and Heron Lakes, or the Birding Center near the Arboretum Trail at Triangle Lake.

Track the list

See every species reported at the Willow Waterhole eBird hotspot. Binoculars are available to borrow — just email ahead.

A Living Classroom

Learn just by showing up.

Monthly bird walks, family fishing clinics, foraging and native-plant walks, and hands-on stewardship days make the park a place to learn by doing — at any age. The boardwalk's interpretive panels and outdoor classroom welcome school groups and curious visitors alike. Want to bring a school group, scout troop, or organization? We'd love to hear from you.

See what's coming up.

Bird surveys, workdays, guided walks, and seasonal events — there's a reason to come back every month.
