Greenway History

From stormwater basin to urban oasis.

In 2001, as the Harris County Flood Control District began designing detention basins for the Brays Bayou watershed, the Brays Bayou Association founded the Willow Waterhole Greenspace Conservancy. The vision was bold: build a major public park on top of the flood-control infrastructure.

Over the next two decades, the community brought it to life, excavating six lakes, planting native trees, building trails, and dedicating gazebos, memorials, and gardens. By 2026, full flood-control functionality and eight miles of ADA trails were complete.

What We Do

Flood protection

The greenway is a working stormwater detention system that helps protect surrounding neighborhoods during major storms.

Habitat restoration

We actively manage prairie, wetland, and woodland habitats that support hundreds of plant and animal species.

Recreation & access

Eight miles of accessible trails, six lakes, and gathering spaces, free and open to all every day.

Education & community

Bird surveys, fishing clinics, volunteer days, and events that connect people to nature and each other.

Flood Control

How the lakes reduce flooding.

Every lake you walk beside is doing a second job.

The six lakes of Willow Waterhole are a stormwater detention system, engineered by the Harris County Flood Control District as part of Project Brays, a $480 million effort with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to reduce flood risk across the Brays Bayou watershed. When heavy rain falls, the lakes fill, holding water that would otherwise surge into neighborhood streets and releasing it slowly as the bayou recedes.

The system reduces flooding. It doesn't eliminate it. Storms larger than the design event, like Hurricane Harvey, can still overwhelm any detention system. But in storm after storm, these basins have quietly done exactly what they were built to do.

The Harris County Flood Control District engineers and maintains the infrastructure; the Conservancy stewards the living greenspace on top of it. That partnership is the whole idea of Willow Waterhole: protection and beauty on the same ground.

Our Partners

We don't do this alone.

Willow Waterhole exists thanks to a partnership of public agencies, foundations, and community organizations, each with a distinct role.

Public Partners

Harris County Flood Control District

Engineered and maintains the detention basins the greenway is built on; manages the prairie preserve under a formal management plan.

Houston Parks & Recreation

The city partner for parkland, programming, and public access across the greenway.

City of Houston & TIRZ 20

Civic partners supporting infrastructure, access, and neighborhood investment around the park. TIRZ 20 annexed Willow Waterhole Greenway in late 2023.

Brays Oaks Management District

A district partner supporting safety, beautification, and community connection in southwest Houston.

Texas Parks & Wildlife

Stocked four lakes with bass, catfish, and sunfish, and funded the Prairie Boardwalk through a 2023 grant.
